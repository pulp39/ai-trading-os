# P-20260310-006

## Title

Candidate Hypothesis Formation Framework for the Proposer Layer

## Status

draft (revised)

## Proposal Type

research_hypothesis / institutional_framework

---

## Scope Statement

This proposal defines a candidate framework governing how the Proposer layer should construct research hypotheses from structured observation payload records. It addresses four open institutional questions identified in the research context preceding this proposal:

1. How the Proposer layer should construct hypotheses from observation payloads
2. Whether hypotheses should reference individual observations or sets of observations
3. How hypothesis confidence should relate to signal quality classification
4. How hypotheses should be structured so that they are testable and revisable

This proposal does not modify the observation payload structure defined in P-20260310-003, the parameter specification defined in P-20260310-004, or the signal quality classification framework defined in P-20260310-005. It operates above those layers, describing how their outputs are consumed by the Proposer.

This proposal is advisory. It does not authorize execution, data collection, or system modification.

---

## Purpose

The institution now has a complete and accepted observation layer. It defines what to observe, how to measure it, and how to classify the reliability of what was recorded. What the institution does not yet have is a defined method for transforming those structured observations into research hypotheses.

Without such a framework, hypothesis formation risks becoming ad hoc — hypotheses may be generated from single observations rather than from observation sets, confidence levels may be asserted without reference to signal quality, and hypotheses may be stated in ways that cannot be meaningfully tested or revised. Over time this would degrade the intellectual quality of the proposal layer and undermine the institution's core purpose of knowledge accumulation.

This proposal defines the minimum structural requirements for a well-formed research hypothesis within AI Trading OS, and describes the process by which the Proposer layer should move from observation payload records to a hypothesis suitable for institutional debate.

---

## Research Hypothesis

> If the Proposer layer constructs hypotheses according to a defined formation framework — referencing observation sets rather than individual records, grounding confidence levels in signal quality classification, and structuring hypotheses in testable and revisable form — then the quality and durability of proposals under Librarian challenge will improve systematically over successive research cycles.

This hypothesis is itself subject to institutional testing over time. It is offered as a founding assumption of the framework rather than an established finding.

---

## Core Principles of Hypothesis Formation

The framework rests on four principles that follow directly from the institution's constitutional commitments.

**Principle 1 — Observation grounding**
Every hypothesis must be traceable to one or more observation payload records. A hypothesis that cannot be linked to specific trace_event observations is not an institutional research hypothesis — it is speculation. Traceability is a constitutional requirement and applies to hypothesis formation as much as to observation recording.

**Principle 2 — Set-based reasoning**
Hypotheses should be constructed from observation sets rather than individual records wherever possible. A single observation may reflect noise, data error, or a non-repeating condition. An observation set — a collection of records sharing defined characteristics — provides a more stable foundation for hypothesis generation. The minimum set size is addressed in Stage 1 below.

**Principle 3 — Quality-conditioned confidence**
Hypothesis confidence must be explicitly conditioned on the signal quality classification of the supporting observation set. Confidence is bounded by the lowest reliability tier present in the observation set. A set containing even one `minimal` record cannot support a `High` or `Moderate-High` confidence claim regardless of the quality of the remaining records. This rule prevents overconfidence when mixed quality observations are present.

**Principle 4 — Testability and revisability**
A well-formed hypothesis must specify the conditions under which it would be confirmed, the conditions under which it would be disconfirmed, and the observation payload dimensions most relevant to its evaluation. A hypothesis that cannot in principle be disconfirmed is not a research hypothesis — it is an assertion. The framework requires that all hypotheses declare their disconfirmation conditions explicitly.

## Hypothesis Type Classification

Before constructing a hypothesis, the Proposer should identify its type. Hypothesis type determines which payload dimensions are expected to be most relevant and conditions the selection criteria used in Stage 1.

Hypothesis types:

- **Pattern Hypothesis**
  A claim that a recurring configuration of payload dimension values is associated with a subsequent observable outcome. Pattern hypotheses are primarily grounded in price context, volume context, and volatility dimensions.

- **Regime Hypothesis**
  A claim that the market is operating within a structurally distinct behavioral state that conditions the interpretation of other observations. Regime hypotheses are primarily grounded in the volatility context and correlation state dimensions.

- **Causal Hypothesis**
  A claim that a specific identifiable condition — such as macro event proximity — exerts directional influence on subsequent payload observations. Causal hypotheses are primarily grounded in the macro proximity dimension but may implicate all five payload dimensions.

Hypothesis type is recorded in the proposal and informs the dimensional basis declaration in Stage 4.

## Hypothesis Formation Process

The formation process defines six stages the Proposer should follow when constructing a hypothesis from observation payload records.

---

### Stage 1 — Observation Set Assembly

The Proposer assembles a set of observation payload records relevant to the research question under investigation. The set must be defined by explicit selection criteria, which become part of the proposal record.

**Selection criteria may include:**
- Time range (e.g., observations from a defined calendar period)
- Asset and timeframe (per the parameter specification in P-20260310-004)
- Signal quality tier filter (e.g., `complete` and `partial` records only)
- Dimensional conditions (e.g., observations where `volatility_context.ratio_short_to_long > 1.0`)

**Observation set traceability requirement**: For each record included in the observation set, the Proposer must record the following identifiers:

- `observation_id` — the unique identifier of the observation payload record
- `trace_event_id` — the identifier of the trace_event entry containing the payload
- `signal_quality_tier` — the quality tier assigned to the record per P-20260310-005

These three identifiers anchor each hypothesis to the institutional trace_event record and satisfy the traceability principle of the constitution.

**Minimum set size**: A set of fewer than five observation records is proposed as insufficient for hypothesis generation under normal conditions. A single-observation hypothesis may be submitted for institutional debate but must be explicitly flagged as speculative and assigned a confidence level of `Speculative` regardless of the quality tier of the single record.

---

### Stage 2 — Quality Profile Assessment

Before constructing a hypothesis, the Proposer assesses the quality profile of the assembled observation set using the signal quality classification defined in P-20260310-005.

The quality profile of an observation set is described by:

| Metric | Definition |
|---|---|
| `complete_count` | Number of records with tier `complete` |
| `partial_count` | Number of records with tier `partial` |
| `minimal_count` | Number of records with tier `minimal` |
| `unreliable_count` | Number of records with tier `unreliable` |
| `lowest_tier_present` | The lowest quality tier present across all records in the set |
| `dominant_tier` | The tier held by the majority of records in the set |
| `implied_volatility_coverage` | Proportion of records where implied volatility is populated |

The quality profile is recorded in the proposal and directly governs the confidence ceiling applied in Stage 5.

**Unreliable records**: Records with tier `unreliable` must be excluded from hypothesis formation sets. They may be referenced in reliability notes but must not contribute to the observational foundation of a hypothesis.

---

### Stage 3 — Hypothesis Type Classification

The Proposer identifies the hypothesis type from the classification defined above:

- Pattern Hypothesis
- Regime Hypothesis
- Causal Hypothesis

The type is recorded in the proposal. If a hypothesis spans multiple types, the Proposer should identify the primary type and note the secondary type. Mixed-type hypotheses carry additional complexity and should be noted as such in the confidence statement.

---

### Stage 4 — Hypothesis Construction

The Proposer constructs the hypothesis from the assembled and quality-assessed observation set. A well-formed hypothesis within this framework must contain the following elements:

**4a — Hypothesis statement**
A declarative claim about a relationship, pattern, or dynamic observed in the payload data. The statement must be specific enough to be evaluated against future observations.

*Example form*: "When [dimensional condition X] is observed across [N] consecutive daily observation payloads for equity index futures, [outcome Y] occurs in the subsequent observation with frequency greater than baseline."

**4b — Observation set reference**
An explicit reference to the observation set from which the hypothesis was formed, including the selection criteria defined in Stage 1, the traceability identifiers (`observation_id`, `trace_event_id`, `signal_quality_tier`) for each record, and the quality profile assessed in Stage 2.

**4c — Dimensional basis**
An identification of which payload dimensions defined in P-20260310-003 are most relevant to the hypothesis, and which are not materially implicated. This allows future observation sets to be constructed with targeted completeness requirements.

**4d — Confirmation condition**
A specification of the observation pattern that would constitute evidence in favor of the hypothesis. The condition must be expressible in terms of payload dimensions and parameter values.

**4e — Disconfirmation condition**
A specification of the observation pattern that would constitute evidence against the hypothesis. This is a mandatory element. A hypothesis without a disconfirmation condition does not meet the institutional standard for a testable research artifact.

**4f — Baseline reference**
A description of the baseline against which the hypothesized pattern is being compared. A hypothesis that claims a pattern occurs "frequently" without defining a baseline frequency is not well-formed.

---

### Stage 5 — Confidence Formation

The confidence statement must be constructed according to the following rules. Confidence is bounded by the lowest reliability tier present in the observation set as assessed in Stage 2.

| Lowest tier present in set | Maximum permissible confidence level |
|---|---|
| `complete`, set size ≥ 10 | High |
| `complete`, set size 5–9 | Moderate-High |
| `partial`, set size ≥ 10 | Moderate |
| `partial`, set size 5–9 | Moderate-Low |
| `minimal`, any set size | Low |
| Single-observation set, any tier | Speculative |

**Confidence ceiling rule**: The confidence level assigned must not exceed the maximum permissible level for the lowest tier present. A set where the majority of records are `complete` but one record is `minimal` is bounded at `Low`. This rule is strict. It prevents mixed-quality observation sets from supporting confidence levels that their weakest records do not warrant.

The Proposer may assign a lower confidence level than the ceiling if additional epistemic uncertainty warrants it. The Proposer may not assign a higher level under any circumstances.

---

### Stage 6 — Revision Conditions Declaration

Every hypothesis must declare the conditions under which the Proposer would consider revising or withdrawing the hypothesis. This is distinct from the disconfirmation condition, which describes what the data would need to show. The revision conditions declaration describes what institutional events would prompt the Proposer to reconsider the hypothesis.

**Revision condition types:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Observational revision trigger | A specification of how many disconfirming observations, at what quality tier, would prompt hypothesis revision |
| Parameter revision trigger | A note that changes to the parameter specification in P-20260310-004 may affect the hypothesis and would require reassessment |
| Schema revision trigger | A note that changes to the payload structure in P-20260310-003 that affect the hypothesis's dimensional basis would require reassessment |

## Hypothesis Record Structure

The following illustrates how a hypothesis formed under this framework would be represented as a structured element within a proposal:
```
hypothesis:
  type: [Pattern | Regime | Causal]
  statement: [declarative claim]

  observation_set:
    selection_criteria: [explicit criteria used to assemble the set]
    set_size: [number of records]
    records:
      - observation_id: [identifier]
        trace_event_id: [identifier]
        signal_quality_tier: [complete | partial | minimal]
    quality_profile:
      complete_count: [numeric]
      partial_count: [numeric]
      minimal_count: [numeric]
      lowest_tier_present: [complete | partial | minimal]
      dominant_tier: [complete | partial | minimal]
      implied_volatility_coverage: [numeric, 0.0–1.0]

  dimensional_basis:
    primary_dimensions: [list of payload dimensions most relevant to hypothesis]
    secondary_dimensions: [list of payload dimensions peripherally relevant]
    uninvolved_dimensions: [list of payload dimensions not materially implicated]

  confirmation_condition: [observable pattern in payload terms]
  disconfirmation_condition: [observable pattern in payload terms]
  baseline_reference: [description of baseline frequency or condition]

  confidence:
    level: [High | Moderate-High | Moderate | Moderate-Low | Low | Speculative]
    lowest_tier_present: [tier driving the confidence ceiling]
    quality_basis: [reference to quality profile justifying the level]
    additional_qualifications: [any further epistemic caveats]

  revision_conditions:
    observational_trigger: [description]
    parameter_trigger: [description]
    schema_trigger: [description]
```

## Relationship to the Proposal Object

The hypothesis record structure defined above is a structured element within a proposal, not a replacement for the proposal object. A proposal may contain one or more hypothesis records alongside the other institutional elements that constitute the full artifact:
```
proposal
├── metadata (proposal_id, title, status, date, author)
├── purpose and context
├── hypothesis type classification
├── one or more hypothesis records (structured per this framework)
├── open research questions
├── confidence statement (summarizing across hypotheses if multiple)
├── limitations
└── future proposal directions
```

## Open Research Questions

1. Should the minimum observation set size of five records be treated as a fixed institutional rule or a default that Proposers may justify departing from with explicit reasoning?
2. Should the confidence ceiling rules defined in Stage 5 be subject to periodic review as the institution accumulates observation data and can assess their calibration empirically?
3. When a proposal contains multiple hypotheses with different quality profiles and confidence levels, how should the overall proposal confidence be summarized? The strict reading of the ceiling rule would apply the lowest tier across all hypotheses.
4. Should the Librarian be empowered to challenge a proposal solely on the grounds that its observation set does not meet the minimum size or quality tier requirements, independently of the intellectual content of the hypothesis?

## Confidence Statement

**Confidence level**: Moderate-High

The framework is internally consistent and maps cleanly onto the observation layer defined in proposals 003 through 005. The six-stage formation process preserves constitutional role boundaries and the traceability principle. The confidence ceiling rule provides a deterministic and strict link between observation quality and hypothesis confidence that prevents discretionary inflation.

The lowest-confidence elements are the minimum observation set size of five records and the confidence ceiling thresholds in Stage 5. Both are proposed as institutional conventions rather than empirically derived values and are expected to require revision once the institution has accumulated sufficient observation data.

---

## Limitations

- This framework does not specify how hypotheses are evaluated after formation. Evaluation procedures are deferred to a future proposal.
- The framework does not address multi-asset or multi-timeframe hypotheses, deferred pending extension of the parameter specification beyond the daily equity index futures context of P-20260310-004.
- The minimum set size and confidence ceiling thresholds are candidate values not yet validated against institutional observation data.
- This proposal is advisory. It does not authorize any execution, data collection, or system modification.
